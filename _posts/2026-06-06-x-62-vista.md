Most people know the X-62A VISTA as the F-16 that flies itself—the testbed where AI agents dogfought human pilots and won the trust of the Air Force. That story is real, and it matters. But it misses what makes VISTA truly strange, and truly useful: the X-62 is modified to test artificial intelligence and autonomy capabilities that allow it to mimic the performance characteristics of other aircraft. Not just any aircraft. *Any* aircraft a researcher wants to test against.

The Variable Stability System operates in parallel with the normal F-16 control laws and commands, with essentially full-authority, the left and right horizontal stabilizer, ailerons, thrust, rudder, and trailing edge flaps. The front seat pilot flies the F-16 through the second control system and feels like he was flying the simulated aircraft. In the air, the jet's natural aerodynamics are overridden by software. The pilot's stick inputs are interpreted not by the F-16's own flight control computer, but by a programmable layer that can make the jet respond like something else entirely.

VISTA's ability to replicate the flight characteristics of other aircraft while physically remaining an F-16 is enabled by the VISTA Simulation System, developed by Calspan, alongside Lockheed Martin's model-following flight control algorithms, allowing the jet's natural aerodynamics to be overridden through software. Want to test an AI agent against an F-22? Load the Raptor's flight model. Need a Su-35 red-air adversary without actually acquiring a Flanker? VISTA can fly like one. Want to see how your algorithm handles a 1950s-era F-104 Starfighter, or a notional sixth-generation platform that exists only as equations in a Skunk Works lab? VISTA can do that, too.

This is not Hollywood shape-shifting. It's a second flight control system—what Calspan calls the Variable Stability System—that intercepts pilot commands and control surface deflections in real time, reshaping the aircraft's dynamic response to match a target model. The NF-16D VISTA testbed aircraft incorporated a multi-axis thrust vectoring engine nozzle that provides for more active control of the aircraft in a post-stall situation; as a result, the aircraft is supermaneuverable, retaining pitch and yaw control at angles of attack beyond which the traditional control surfaces cannot change attitude. That thrust-vectoring nozzle gives VISTA an envelope wider than a stock Viper's, which means it can credibly emulate jets with radically different handling—high-alpha monsters, or twitchy lightweight designs that would snap a conventional F-16's wing off.

The implications are tactical, not just technical. Initial testing of the AI-controlled X-62A against human fighter pilots began in late 2023; in September 2023 the X-62 flew against a live manned F-16, building up in safety using maneuvers first defensive, then offensive, and then high aspect nose to nose engagements where the aircraft got as close as 2,000 feet at 1,200 miles per hour. But those engagements weren't just AI versus human. They were AI learning to fight *any simulated adversary the test team programmed*—including threats that don't exist yet. VISTA doesn't just test whether an autonomous agent can dogfight. It tests whether that agent can dogfight an F-35. Or a J-20. Or a fictional aggressor with flight characteristics pulled from intelligence estimates and loaded into the VSS before takeoff.

Originally designated NF-16D, the aircraft was redesignated X-62A in June 2021, marking its transition from a test pilot training tool to a national-level AI research asset. Calspan has developed several generations of in-flight simulator aircraft culminating in the USAF VISTA NF-16D based at the USAF Test Pilot School at Edwards Air Force Base; today, Calspan provides maintenance, operations support, engineering, and curriculum development for the USAF TPS VISTA/F-16. The aircraft remains on the Test Pilot School syllabus, but its mission has expanded: it's now the Air Force's primary platform for testing autonomous combat algorithms in contested, high-speed scenarios that ground simulators can't replicate.

The X-62 doesn't replace the need for actual fifth-gen adversary aircraft or live red-air exercises. But it does something those can't: it lets you fight tomorrow's threat today, by programming VISTA to *be* that threat. The AI doesn't just learn to dogfight. It learns to dogfight a moving target—one that can be reprogrammed between sorties, or even mid-flight, to test adaptability under uncertainty.

VISTA's variable-stability concept isn't new—Calspan has been building these systems since the 1940s. But pairing that capability with machine-learning autonomy and thrust-vectoring supermaneuverability creates something genuinely novel: a programmable opponent generator. The X-62 is an F-16 that can cosplay as anything with wings. And in an era when the next air war may be fought against adversaries we haven't met yet, that's not a gimmick. It's a strategic hedge.
