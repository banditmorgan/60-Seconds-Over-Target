On May 2, 2024, Secretary of the Air Force Frank Kendall flew in the front seat of the X-62A VISTA at Edwards Air Force Base. The flight itself was not remarkable—the AI-controlled F-16 flew Kendall in lightning-fast maneuvers at more than 550 miles an hour that put pressure on his body at five times the force of gravity, going nearly nose to nose with a second human-piloted F-16 as both aircraft raced within 1,000 feet of each other. Test pilots had been doing this for months. What mattered was who was in the cockpit, and what he did once he got there.

Kendall had a button on his stick where he initiated the automation. He pressed it roughly a dozen times during the hour-long flight, each time transferring control of a supersonic fighter jet to an algorithm. Neither Secretary Kendall nor the safety pilot in the backseat touched the controls of the X-62A during the entire test flight. And when he climbed out of the cockpit, he said he'd seen enough during his flight that he'd trust this still-learning AI with the ability to decide whether or not to launch weapons in war.

That statement—from a Cabinet-level official who controls a $173 billion annual budget and the acquisition strategy for the entire Air Force—is the story. The X-62A's technical milestone had already happened. The VISTA was flown by an AI-driven autonomy agent for more than 17 hours during 12 flight tests at Edwards AFB in December 2022, and in April 2024, the USAF and DARPA announced the successful engagement of the X-62A against a conventional, human piloted F-16 in the first-ever human vs artificial intelligence dogfight. Kendall's flight was a demonstration of trust, not capability.

## The Platform

The X-62 VISTA ("Variable Stability In-flight Simulator Test Aircraft") is an experimental aircraft, derived from the F-16D Fighting Falcon. Originally designated NF-16D, the aircraft was redesignated X-62A in June 2021 as part of an upgrade to a Skyborg, with System for Autonomous Control of Simulation (SACS). The airframe started life as an F-16D Block 30, was modified with multi-axis thrust vectoring for post-stall control, and has been a workhorse of the Air Force Test Pilot School curriculum since 1992.

The AI-driven autonomy agents of AFRL's Autonomous Air Combat Operations (AACO) and DARPA's Air Combat Evolution (ACE) piloted the VISTA X-62A for the exercises. ACE applies existing artificial intelligence technologies to the dogfight problem in experiments of increasing realism, and implements methods to measure, calibrate, increase, and predict human trust in combat autonomy performance. The brief for the program is explicit: prove that AI can handle within-visual-range combat—the most dynamic, high-speed decision environment in military aviation—so that pilots can trust it with lower-level tactical execution while they manage the broader fight.

## The Man Who Pressed the Button

Frank Kendall is an engineer, lawyer and executive who served as the 26th United States secretary of the Air Force from 2021 to 2025, a West Point graduate who retired as a lieutenant colonel from the U.S. Army Reserve and served from 2011 to 2017 as the under secretary of defense for acquisition, technology and logistics in the Obama administration. He is not a fighter pilot. He does not need to prove his courage. His job is to decide what the Air Force buys, and whether it works.

When Kendall says he would trust the AI to make weapons-release decisions, he is not speaking as a passenger impressed by a smooth ride. He is speaking as the civilian leader responsible for fielding the systems that will define air combat for the next generation. The performance of the modified fighter jet was nearly on par with the abilities of an experienced F-16 pilot with two or three thousand hours of experience during recent dogfighting exercises, Kendall reported. The AI held its own.

## What It Means

The X-62A is not a prototype weapon. The U.S. Air Force Test Pilot School's research division oversees the comprehensive program management for the X-62A, and the aircraft remains a testbed. But the work being done on it is not academic. The Air Force is planning for an AI-enabled fleet of more than 1,000 unmanned warplanes, the first of them operating by 2028.

The technical risk is well understood. The X-62A is flown with safety pilots onboard with the independent ability to disengage the AI agent should something go sideways. The harder problem—the one Kendall's flight was designed to address—is trust. Can a human commander delegate life-and-death decisions to a machine that learns, rather than one that simply executes pre-programmed rules? Kendall's answer, after a dozen button presses and an hour of watching the algorithm fight, was yes.

That is the inflection point. Not the first AI dogfight. Not the first autonomous landing or the first simulated weapons pass. The moment when the man who decides what the Air Force fields decided that the AI was ready to be trusted with the hardest call a pilot makes. The button press was simple. The decision it represents is not.
