# The Safety Pilot Who Never Had to Intervene

December 2022. Edwards Air Force Base. A test pilot climbs into the back seat of the X-62A VISTA, hand ready on the override switch. His job, for the next twelve flights, is to expect the AI to fail.

It doesn't.

Between December 1 and 16, 2022, AI agents successfully piloted the X-62A for the first time across 12 flights, marking the beginning of DARPA's Air Combat Evolution (ACE) program—an effort to increase trust in combat autonomy by using human-machine collaborative dogfighting as its challenge problem. The flights weren't gentle shakedown runs. Two AI agents—the Air Force Research Lab's Autonomous Air Combat Operations (AACO) and DARPA's ACE—performed different roles: AACO handled one-on-one beyond-visual-range engagements against simulated adversaries, while ACE executed within-visual-range dogfighting maneuvers.

The safety pilot's role was straightforward: watch the machine, trust nothing, and disengage the instant things went sideways. A safety pilot was on board the VISTA aircraft to take control if anything went awry. But across those twelve flights, the switch stayed silent. The AI held the aircraft through maneuvers that pushed the edge of the envelope, and the human in the back seat—trained to intervene—never had to.

## From Simulation to Steel

In less than three years, AI algorithms developed under DARPA's ACE program progressed from controlling simulated F-16s on computer screens to controlling an actual F-16 in flight, with ACE developers uploading their AI software into the X-62A in early December 2022. The X-62A itself is no ordinary test aircraft. Originally designated NF-16D, the aircraft—a modified F-16D developed jointly by General Dynamics and Calspan—was redesignated X-62A in June 2021 as part of an upgrade that added the System for Autonomous Control of Simulation (SACS).

The December 2022 flights were a proof of concept, but they set the stage for something harder. A total of 21 test flights were conducted for the project between December 2022 and September 2023, and more than 100,000 lines of flight-critical software changes were made over time to improve the tools. By September 2023, the program had advanced to live engagements against human-piloted F-16s. The aircraft closed to as near as 2,000 feet at 1,200 miles per hour in high-aspect nose-to-nose engagements, building up through defensive maneuvers before progressing to offensive scenarios.

While safety pilots were onboard with the ability to disengage the AI, they never had to activate the safety switch during the dogfights.

## The Trust Problem

The technical challenge of teaching an AI to dogfight is significant—reaction times measured in milliseconds, physics at the edge of human tolerance, decisions with no room for second-guessing. But the pilot must be able to trust the autonomy to conduct complex combat behaviors in scenarios such as the within visual range dogfight, and that's a different problem entirely.

ACE implemented methods to measure, calibrate, increase, and predict human trust in combat autonomy performance. The program's goal wasn't just to prove the AI could fly—it was to prove humans could let it. The trials mark a signature victory for DARPA's Air Combat Evolution program, which is seeking to increase human trust in autonomous platforms that the military services hope to deploy in combat.

The results influenced policy at the highest levels. Air Force Secretary Frank Kendall has said the successes of the ACE program contributed to his decision to move ahead with the Air Force's collaborative combat aircraft (CCA) program, an effort to field autonomous drones as loyal wingmen to manned fighters. In May 2024, Kendall flew in the X-62A himself—and afterward stated he'd trust the AI with the ability to launch weapons.

The hardest part of teaching AI to fight wasn't writing the software. It was teaching the pilot in the back seat not to reach for the switch.
